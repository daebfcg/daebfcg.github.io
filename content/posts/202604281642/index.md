## Fret Preparation

First we make each fret.

This consists of:

1. Bending to the correct radius
1. Cutting each fret to the correct length
1. Removing the end of the tang that overlaps the fretboard binding
1. Pre-dressing the ends

I am using pre-bent fretwire, so step 1 can be skipped.  I have used fretwire from 2 foot straight lengths as well as long coiled pieces, and getting it pre-radiused is the way to go.  This is from Philadelphia Luthier Tools who sells packs of 25 pre-bent to a 12 inch radius for close to the same cost as buying in bulk.

{{<imgresize fretwire.jpg "800x800" "Pre-Bent Fretwire">}}

This guitar is getting 47x104 Jescar stainless steel.

## Cut to length and tang removal

This is easily done with a Dremel and metal-cutting disk.

{{<imgresize cutting_wheel.jpg "800x800" "Dremel cutting wheel">}}

{{<imgresize tang_cut.jpg "800x800" "Tang removed">}}

Each fret is cut to length and de-tanged.  Some light filing on the bottom where the tang was removed is usually needed.

{{<imgresize fret_cut.jpg "800x800" "Fret cut to length and de-tanged">}}

The prepared frets are kept organized in a holder.

{{<imgresize frets_prepared.jpg "800x800" "Frets kept in a holder">}}

## End Dressing

I pre-dress the ends of my frets before installing.

First I pre-file each corner to a 45 degree angle, then come in over the top with the fret-crowning file also at a 45 degree angle.  This is just to give the next step a head-start.

{{<imgresize file_ends.jpg "800x800" "Filing the fret ends">}}

{{<imgresize file_top.jpg "800x800" "Filing the end crown of the fret">}}

Then the semi-hemispherical shape is ground smooth on a special emery polishing wheel in the Dremel.  This concave-edged wheel was made by running it over a small round file.  

{{<imgresize polishing_wheel.jpg "800x800" "Polishing wheel with concave edge">}}

Just a couple seconds in this and the fret ends come out remarkably round and smooth with no rough edges at all.

{{<imgresize frets_prepared_detail.jpg "800x800" "Closeup of prepared frets with ends pre-dressed">}}

## Installation

With the frets prepared to this extent, installation is a breeze.

First a bead of hide glue is spread into the fret slot.  Then wiped off flush with DRY paper towel.

{{<imgresize glue_1.jpg "800x800" "Putting hide glue in fret slot">}}

(Notice how I accidentally skipped a fret trying to get a photo of the process...)

{{<imgresize glue_2.jpg "800x800" "Hide glue in fret slot">}}

{{<imgresize glue_3.jpg "800x800" "Glue wiped clean">}}

The fret is then positioned and lightly pressed (not pounded) in with a soft mallet just enough to get it to stay put.

{{<imgresize hammer.jpg "800x800" "Fret pushed in to position">}}

The fret is seated home using a fret-press caul mounted in the drill press.

{{<imgresize fret_pressing.jpg "800x800" "Fret Press">}}

Lastly, the excess glue is wiped off with a warm wet rag.

{{<imgresize fretting_1.jpg "800x800" "Installing Frets">}}

## Fretboard finishing

I let the hide glue dry about four hours and lightly sand the fretboard between the frets to 600 (to knock back the raised grain from being wetted) , then get the first coat of tung oil on the fretboard.  This stuff takes several days to cure between coats.

The first coat is a 50/50 mix of pure tung oil and food-grade D-limonene.  The thinning this way helps the first coat penetrate much better (and dry faster).  The second coat will be straight tung oil.
