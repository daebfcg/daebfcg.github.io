This is a minimalist French Polishing.

The instrument was previously sanded to 1000 grit and has one seal-coat of 1-pound-cut shellac.  I use 1-pound cut for everything.

{{<imgresize first_session.jpg "800x800" "First finishing session">}}

This seal coat stays on for the initial string up and test-playing stage.  When it is time for the final finish to be applied, the process goes like this:

1. Lightly sand to 1000 grit (dry).  Any trouble spots or noticed scratches are sanded with 800-400 grit if needed, and down to 1000 again.

1. Apply around seven light coats of shellac in continuous succession.  Sometimes called a "bodying" session.  Rub out with alcohol.

1. Cure for around 72 hours.

1. Wet sand with 1000 grit, using walnut oil as the wetting/de-clogging medium.  Clean off all oil and dust with naphtha (Zippo fluid).

1. Apply another 5 or so coats of shellac, and rub out with alcohol again.

1. Cure for 72 hours.

1. Wet sand to 3000 grit with walnut oil and clean off with naphtha.

1. Apply one light top coat of shellac.

1. Cure for 72 - 96 hours before handling.

## Stand

I devised a simple way to hold the guitar while finishing.  This takes advantage of two places that do not need to be finished and will have hardware covering them in the end:  the tuner holes and the holes for the bridge/string-through ferrules.  The posts have little rubber bolt-caps on them, which work well for preventing slipping but are soft enough not to mar.

## Tools

The one tool for shellacing is the muñeca, aka "rubber", aka pad.  This is made by tightly wrapping a wad of cheesecloth in a square of old cotton t-shirt.  Drops of shellac are applied to the muñeca using a pipette to "charge" it.

A small dropper bottle of spirit (Everclear) is used for the final rub out of a bodying session.

## Curing

Between sessions, the shellac needs to cure for a few days.  I typically use a mic stand, but this solid body guitar is too heavy so I hung it on my door.
