## Template

The template for drilling the holes was made in Libre Office Draw by taking a photo of the original control cavity cutout template, sizing to 100%, and then arranging models of the components to fit.  The component models were made to their actual size to ensure everything will fit within the boundary of the cavity without touching or overlapping each other.

{{<imgresize making_template.png "600x600" "Creating the template">}}

Once I got the layout determined, I printed out the template and did a test run on a sheet of mdf.  The mdf sheet was cut into the shape of the control cavity to double-check that none of the components would be going past the edges.  This also gave me a chance to check the functionality of the layout and make sure things were not too close together and each switch and knob could be easily reached and actuated.

{{<imgresize test_plate_front.jpg "600x600" "Controls installed into the test plate">}}

{{<imgresize test_plate_rear.jpg "600x600" "Inside view of the controls in the test plate">}}

This took me four tries to arrive at an arrangement that I was happy with.

## Drilling the holes

One challenge was making sure that the template was positioned correctly on the top the guitar in relation to the inside cavity.  Fortunately I saved the original template cutout of the cavity and used it as a guide to draw light pencil lines on the face of the guitar representing the cavity.  Positioning this took some careful measuring and estimating, and finally verified by using a rare earth magnet on the side with a small screw on the top to ensure the lines were actually in the correct place.

{{<imgresize template_paper.jpg "600x600" "Paper template placed on the guitar">}}

I then drilled 3/64 pilot holes, and drilled to their final size using forstener bits.   3/8 inch for pots, 1/2 inch for the switches.

{{<imgresize pilot_holes.jpg "600x600" "Pilot holes">}}

I made sure to get clean holes without any tear out by starting the hole from the inside and then finishing from the front.

{{<imgresize drilling.jpg "800x800" "Drilling the holes">}}

## A Major Screw Up

### A stupid and easily avoidable mistake

While drilling from the inside, I accidentally went all the way through on one hole and ended up blowing out wood around the hole in the top.

I should have pre-drilled from the top first, then the back, and finally all the way through the top.  That would have prevented this error.

{{<imgresize holes.jpg "600x600" "Holes drilled">}}

I need to fix this.

## Fixing a Hole

A few options for dealing with the blow-out around the hole

1. Sand it out and deal with the resulting indentation
1. Sand the entire front of guitar down past the depth of the missing chunk.
1. Glue the missing chunk back in and sand smooth
1. Patch in some filler shavings and sand smooth

Did not want #1.  #2 seems unfeasible considering the depth of the gouge.   I would like to try #3, although I think I lost the chunk of wood that got blown out, unfortunately.  I have some chunks that are close, so will try that.

{{<imgresize hole_fix_chips.jpg "800x800" "Chips of matching wood for gouged area">}}

This patch chunk was hide-glued in and clamped down.   I may have had better results using some shavings as they might confirm the the gouge better.

{{<imgresize hole_fix_clamp.jpg "800x800" "Clamping patch in place">}}

Once the glued cured I chiseled away the paper and extra wood.

{{<imgresize hole_fix_glued.jpg "800x800" "Patch glued">}}

Then sanded smooth.  

{{<imgresize hole_fix_sanded.jpg "800x800" "Patch sanded smooth">}}

Of course it does not look perfect but at least the top surface is flat with no gouged out area.  I think it will look like variations in the wood grain and will not be very noticeable.  The nut that screws down over the switch bushing might cover this up a little, too.

Finally, this half of the guitar top was sanded down to bare wood again, and re-sealed with shellac.

Crisis averted.  You can see it if you look for it, but nobody will see if from six feet away.  Best case scenario is that I forget all about this.

{{<imgresize hole_fix_sealed.jpg "800x800" "Seal coat of shellac reapplied">}}

Next the final stages of finishing can be completed.
