The general procedure is simple - remove everything that does not look like a neck.

## Holding Jig

A special _cruciform_ holding jig that can be firmly held in the vise is made to support the guitar for neck carving.

{{<imgresize cruciform.jpg "800x800" "The cruciform holder jig">}}

Some additional 1/4 mdf and padding is added to the back to prop up the body since the fretboard is already attached.

{{<imgresize cruciform_padding.jpg "800x800" "The cruciform holder jig with padding to account for fretboard">}}

The guitar firmly held face down to the cruciform with three cam clamps.

{{<imgresize on_cruciform.jpg "800x800" "The guitar attached to the cruciform holder">}}

## Taper

The sides of the (currently square) neck are tapered to match the fretboard using a block plane.

On a neck that is being made unattached to the body, the bandsaw is better suited.  In this case, the headstock and body are in the way.

{{<imgresize taper_planing.jpg "800x800" "Taping sides with block plane">}}

{{<imgresize tapered.jpg "600x600" "Sides of neck tapered">}}

{{<imgresize tapered_2.jpg "600x600" "Sides of neck tapered">}}

## Thickness

The target final thickness of the neck is approximately 3/4 inch.  It is currently 1 - 1.5 inch thick (since it extends into the body), so a significant amount of wood will need to be removed.

I made some cross cuts to the depth and sawed the out the excess with the Ryoba saw, then cleaned up with a chisel.

{{<imgresize thickness_chiseling.jpg "800x800" "Thickness">}}

{{<imgresize thickness_sawing.jpg "800x800" "Thickness">}}

{{<imgresize thickness_sections.jpg "800x800" "Thickness">}}

To be sure I did not cut too deep, I set up a guide rail using some aluminum angle bar.  _For future reference, leave an additional 1/8 inch thickness._

{{<imgresize thickness_guide.jpg "800x800" "Thickness">}}

{{<imgresize thicknessed.jpg "800x800" "Thickness">}}

This is also usually done on the bandsaw, but not feasible with the body attached.

## Heel

To start the neck shaping I will focus on the neck-to-body transition (i.e. the heel) first.

The plan is to create a smooth transition under the fretting hand, with the curve based on the multi-scale fret angles.

## Shaping

With the headstock and heel areas roughed out, the overall shaping can be done.  

The first tool using is the Shinto rasp, which removes a lot of wood quickly get sthe initial facets cut.

{{<imgresize shaping_1.jpg "800x800" "Neck carving 1">}}

{{<imgresize shaping_2.jpg "800x800" "Neck carving 2">}}

This is followed up with a four-in-hand rasp to further whittle the neck into the rounded shape.

{{<imgresize shaping_3.jpg "800x800" "Neck carving 3">}}

The edges where it meets the fretboard are cut down with a chisel.

{{<imgresize sides_chisel.jpg "800x800" "Using chisel on edges">}}

The thickness is checked along the way.
{{<imgresize thickness_check.jpg "800x800" "Checking thickness">}}

Starting to look like a neck.
{{<imgresize shaping_4.jpg "800x800" "Neck carving 4">}}

## Sanding

To finish up, the entire neck is sanded with 80 and 100 grit paper to remove all rasp marks and perform the last bit of shaping work.

Finally, it is sanded all over with 150 grit to remove any scratches.

{{<imgresize sanded.jpg "800x800" "The neck fully carves and sanded">}}

The cherry wood has some odd dark spots and pitch-pockets.  The crescent shape near the headstock is not a carving mistake, but a dark spot in the wood.
