## Determine Intonation Points

The first thing to do is determine the lengthwise positions for the bridges.  This will be done for the two outermost strings, and the inner four strings will be put in a straight connecting line between them.  I am not going to directly use the measured scale lengths (25.5" low E, and 25" high E), but instead determine the positions empirically so as to include the additional length needed to account for string stretching, etc.  (i.e. "compensation").  The bridge saddles are adjustable for fine tuning the intonation, which is good, but I would like to try to get them close so minimal adjustment is needed.

I set up an arrangement where the two outer e strings are attached to tuners, with a temporary nut and frets at the 0 and 12 position.  The bottom end of the strings are attached to a jig I made for this operation that acts as a tailpiece to hold the strings.  The idea is that I can compare the 12th fret harmonic with the 12th fret fretted note and move the bridge forward or backward until the pitches are equal.  That will give the the intonation point for the string, and thus determine where the bridge should be mounted.

Once this is done for the high and low e strings, a line is drawn from point to point, which should make about a 13-14 degree angle.

I first tried to use the actual bridges, but the tail end of them is too high to fit right.
{{<imgresize bridge_intonation.jpg "800x800" "Testing intonation with bridges">}}

I switched to a small triangular block of wood, which works much better, and is easier to mark the intonation point.

{{<imgresize dummy_bridge_intonation.jpg "800x800" "Testing intonation with wood block">}}

## First Attempt

My first method for this was to get the two outermost bridges screwed in, and then lay out the remaining four in a line and screw them in.  Unfortunately, the planned distance between the outermost strings on this guitar (2.0625") is a bit more than the minimal width these bridges are designed for so there will be some gap between them.  So I cannot just lay them in side to side touching each other.

{{<imgresize outer_bridges_positioned.jpg "800x800" "Outermost bridges positioned">}}

The ideal side to side placement in this case is much like the principle used for string spacing on a nut - the thicker strings get a slightly larger gap between them, so overall the strings come out equally spaced.

I tried calculating the positions taking into account this gap, but it worked out to about 1/100th of an inch extra between the low e, a, and d strings, and 0 gap betwen the treble strings.

I took a shot at this, basically eyeballing the gaps and drilled the 3/32 holes for the screws.

Here is how that worked out.

{{<imgresize first_attempt_result.jpg "600x600" "Result of initial attempt at bridge positioning">}}

It is not terrible, but far from perfect.  The worst part is that the d string bridge is too low, which throws off the line and sort of stands out as not right.  Also the gaps are not nearly what I had hoped.

This is all a result of the way I drilled the screw holes.  

Looking at the holes, it is clear there is some misalignment.

{{<imgresize first_attempt_holes.jpg "800x800" "Holes misaligned">}}

I decided that it is not acceptable, and will need to try again.

## ReDo

This time I worked out exactly where all the holes should go on the computer and printed out a template.  The template has cross-lines whereever a hole should go, and I used a fine pointed scratch awl to pinprick those locations through the paper and into the wood.

First I have to fill in the holes from the first attempt, though.  I made 6 little 3/32 dowels from cherry and CA glued them in, and cut flush.

This technique of twisting some wood through a hole in metal works amazingly well for making small dowels.

## String Holes

Now for the string holes.  These bridges can support either top-load or string-through-body, so I am going to set up for both options.  Again using a pin prick awl, I made the holes along a the line from the outermost strings, and drilled the 1/8 holes with a brad point bit.  

Then used a 5/16 brad point to make the recess for the top-loading option.  

{{<imgresize all_holes_top.jpg "800x800" "Holes for strings">}}

The 1/8 holes need to go all the way through the body for the string-through-body option.  For that I used the pin trick to get the drill bit exactly aligned from the top hole so I could complete the hole from the back.

{{<imgresize drillpress_pin.jpg "800x800" "Drillpress pin alignment">}}

String through holes from the back.

{{<imgresize holes_back.jpg "800x800" "Holes for strings">}}

Some metal string ferrules will be mounted in the back of the guitar where these 1/8 holes are now.  The ferrules have not arrived yet, so I will wait to drill out the recesses for those.

These look to be in good alignment.
