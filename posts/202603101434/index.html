Fretboard Part 2

Fretboard slotted and dotted

2026-3-11 19:34

The fret slots can now be cut.

Typically I will mark the locations on one edge and use a machinist square to scribe the lines for the slots at 90 degrees to the center/edge lines. Since this is a multi-scale guitar I need to take a different approach.

I have a template with rows of holes at the fret locations for various scale lengths which I had made by SendCutSend. The template includes rows for 25 and 25.5 which are what I am using here.

I start by marking off lines for the outermost e strings, drawn at a taper from nut to bridge. The string width at the nut is going to be 1.44, and 2.07 at the bridge. Along these string lines, I use the template to mark off the fret locations using the 25.5 for the low e and the 25 for the high e. In my case I can start with the template at the zero-fret since that is the one perpendicular fret on this guitar. On my previous multi-scale guitar I made the 7th fret perpendicular, which causes a more pronounced fan. I think I might prefer the uppermost frets to be less fanned though.

Then it is just a matter of connecting the dots to scribe the fret slot lines.

Once scribed I can then cut the slots. I started off using a machinist square clamped into position for each fret, but soon settled on using my wooden block jig that has sandpaper on the the bottom to keep it from slipping and a magnet embedded on the saw side to help keep the saw against the fence.

Only one slip causing a minor scratch at the 20th fret, which will get sanded out during tapering.

At this stage, I cut the slots quite deep, almost 2/3 of the way through, since the edges will be covered by binding strips. Better to be too deep than not deep enough when installing frets.

The fret slots are now cut, and the all that is left is a notch for the nut. For this guitar, it will be embedded Fender-style into the fretboard. Since this guitar will have a zero-fret the nut mainly serves to keep the strings spaced correctly. I think it looks good placed back about 5/32 (approximate width of a fret and a half) from the zero-fret. Dyed-black bone nut from StewMac.

Now the fret position marker dots can be inlaid down the center line.

I am using dots made from reconstituted turquoise stone, keeping with the native North American theme of this guitar. These should look striking on the walnut board. At first I was a bit disappointed in the look of these as they are rather dull straight out of the package and not as light-green tinted as I had hoped. After test-polishing one, though, the color got quite deeper and looked more pleasing. I believe they will darken up and take on a more reflective sheen once polished and buffed.

Now to just drill the holes with a 1/4 forstener bit on the drill press and CA-glue them in.
