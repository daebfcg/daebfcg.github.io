Fretboard Part 1

Preparing the fretboard blank

2026-3-9 23:23

Some unusually humid weather lately so I am taking a break from body-part gluing until things dry out again. A good time to work on the fretboard.

Here is the walnut board I start with. Somewhere in there is a fretboard. The grain is a bit diagonal and I would like the grain lines to be straight up and down so I need to determine the best angle for cutting the edges.

I made a paper negative template than I can lay over the board to best see how the grain lines will look and get a sense of where a good center line will be.

Before trimming down the width I resaw off a 3/8 thick slice to use as the fretboard (the piece of the right). The excess piece on the left will be saved for a future project (ukulele?) so I want to preserve the width.

I decided to use walnut for the fretboard instead of the persimmon I was thinking about. The persimmon is more ideal since it has a higher Janka hardness but the board that I have has some defects on one end and not enough length of good wood for this particular guitar. I will save it for a shorter scale length guitar like the next Super K. My plan is to give the walnut fretboard a good treatment of pure tung oil to seal and harden it up after it is glued to the neck and the frets installed. I like the appearance of the walnut, too, and I think it will be a nice contrast with the lighter maple body wood.

Now I can trim off the sides at the desired angles. Masking tape is used to make the lines fool-proof to see when cutting.

Now for some planing to get the surfaces flat and get the thickness down to the target 1/4 inch.

I stop around 9/32 to give a little bit of extra for final sanding and radiusing.

I like to get the edges trued up too, and use this jointing jig with the #7 plane held upside down in a vise. I got this idea from a Pete Howlett video and it works better than a shooting board for jointing tops and backs and such.

Now the fretboard is dimensioned and ready for the fret slots. At this point the edges are parallel to provide a square reference for the center line. The final taper will be made after cutting the slots.

Some figuring and a couple dark spots have emerged after planing. Ideally that would not have happened but I think I can make it work. The swirly grain figure can go right at the 12th fret, and the dark spot in the middle will land right around the 5th fret. Both these places are significant and will have position-marker inlay dots anyway so hopefully will end up looking more like features rather than flaws. The dark spot close to the edge will probably be mostly removed or at least obscured once the board is tapered.
