Super K Top

Selecting and preparing the top for the next Super K guitar

2026-3-24 13:50

I have three Englemann Spruce top sets to pick from. I got these from StewMac over the course of a few years, who sells them as tops for small acoustic guitars and are not very expensive. This is the first time I am using Engelmann Spruce (Picea engelmannii) for an instrument sound board, although I have had good luck using it as bracing in ukuleles. I used Sitka spruce on the first Super K.

The Engelmann is lighter and softer than Sitka and allegedly more complex and nuanced sound-wise, suitable for a light touch as opposed to aggressive strumming. I think it may be a good match for the type of tone and style these guitars lend themselves to - chord-melody, jazzy lead lines, and laid-back comping. This should result in an interesting comparison.

Selection

Picking the wood for the top. I am not quite sure how to go about it - they all have a nice tap tone, altough each one has its own character. I am probably mostly going by visual appearance.

Top 1 looks ok, is fairly clear, although has some barely visible dark spots.

Top 2 is has some interesting grain lines.

Top 3 looks to be from the same log as top 2. It has some weird dark streaks close to the center.

For some reason, I seem to like top number 2. The grain lines seems to be almost guitar shaped!

Jointing

Using the vise-mounted plane jig to get the edges jointed. The idea is to get the seam where no light comes when pressed together edge to edge.

Glue Up

This is my preferred method for joining tops. String and wedges. It is supposedly an old Spanish guitar building technique. (My wedges need to be bigger next time.)

Cut out

The glue line is scraped down and both surfaces sanded to 220. The final location of the outline is determined, using the mould as a guide. I also draw a light pencil line down the center.

The top is cut out on the bandsaw, about 1/16 - 1/8 inch out from the line. The guitar will have binding so cutting close is not a problem. Some useful scraps remain and are saved.

The edges and top surface get a wash coat of shellac to protect the delicate spruce during the rest of the build. This will all be sanded off before finishing. The inside gets no shellac (yet) since it will be a gluing surface.

The shellac also seals the end grain to deter cracks while drying.
