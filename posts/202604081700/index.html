Fretboard Glue Down (The Wrong Way)

First failed attempts at gluing down the fretboard

2026-4-8 10:02

With the truss rod installed the fretboard can now be glued. The location dowels are locking the fretboard in place and should prevent any slip. Or so I thought.

This is my first time trying this clamping approach. Once the fretboard is set into place and the glue grabs, it is flipped face down on to a rigid and flat surface to act as a clamping caul. A strip of cork is placed between the fretboard and the rigid surface to protect and account for any deviations in pressure or flatness. The thinking is that the fretboard and neck should be glued into as flat a position as possible to minimize future movement and need for much fret-leveling.

This huge slab of soapstone is dead flat and seems like the perfect thing.

Then it is just a matter of clamping hard and letting dry for 24 hours.

Fails 1 and 2

The first two attempts resulted in the fretboard slipping off center so were aborted early. I deduced that the 3/16 poplar dowels being used for positioning pins were getting soft from the glue and losing their holding power.

Fail 3

I added two more position dowels, this time using 1/4 inch oak dowels. I also remade the 3/16 poplar dowel pins - four total position pins. The fretboard should NOT move now and seems well locked when fitted dry.

This slipped too. I think it is the clamping.

  • Too much force with the monster C Clamps.
  • The belly carve in the back of the guitar is opposite the body-end of the fretboard so must be clamped there as well, and the curve is causing the clamp to pull to the side.

Unfortunately I did not notice this until too late so I decided to just let it stay put and see how bad it turned out the next day.

Results

The good news is that the soapstone slab did keep the fretboard very flat and made a nice tight joint.

The bad news is that it did indeed slip off center.

The slip is mostly prominent at the body end. It is off there about 1/16-3/32 inches.

See how bad it is off the intended line here.

Looking down the center, it is fairly noticeable that the neck is not straight with the body. The body end of the fretboard is just slightly too far to the right.

This is slight and can possibly be compensated for in a couple ways.

  1. Move the bridge location slightly off center, or
  2. Keep the bridge in the center and let the strings land where they may, letting an outer string (in this case the bass side) veer off the fretboard as it moves away from the nut.

Since the center lines of this guitar are so integral to the design, moving the bridge to one side will look unacceptably bad, as this experiment with a stand-in bridge shows.

I would probably try to finagle this on a guitar where the center line is not so prominent, and just deal with it.

With option 2 the low e string will likely not even be playable. Also, the fretboard dots would not be between the two middle strings, which looks ugly. Furthermore the strings may not easily line up with the pickup pole pieces.

This option is not going work either, as it would highlight the misalignment.

Decision

Remove the fretboard and try again. This option is only viable since I used hide glue.

Next time I will use my proven clamping technique with a wood caul placed on top. It will not have the advantage of being held perfectly flat, but will be easier to see if things are going awry.
